Personas and Storyboarding- Microwave

This assignment explores how different users interact with different public interfaces. This case study looks at a microwave in a residential dorm kitchen, analyzing its interface, and observing how people interact with it. The project looks at three different personas, eventually focusing on two trying to map their emotions, thoughts, actions, and words, and then a storyboard plot of one user using the microwave

Introduction

This project focuses on analyzing user interactions with public microwaves. Through observations and interviews, I identified key usability challenges and developed personas to represent different user needs. The goal is to explore design improvements that enhance microwave usability.

Observations & Key Findings

After conducting interviews, observing users, and deploying contextual inquiries, I noticed the following patterns with users using this microwave:

  • Most users prefer using the +30 sec button rather than manually setting time.
  • Preset functions (e.g., defrost) are rarely trusted or used correctly.
  • Users avoid adjusting power settings due to lack of clarity and also ease.
  • Common issues include uneven heating and confusing button labels.

Personas

Mimi- The Busy College Senior

“I just need my food heated up quick and easy without complications.”

  • Uses the microwave almost daily.
  • Relies on +30 sec button instead of setting a specific time.
  • Never adjusts power settings.

Zach- The Barista & College Senior

“I dont mind exploring the settings, but sometimes I just need it to work fast especially when I am in a rush.”

  • Prefers inputting time manually but often adds +30 sec for adjustments.
  • Occasionally experiments with power settings but finds them unclear.
  • Does not trust the defrost preset.

Lucas- College Student

“I wish this microwave had more visuals or like a touch screen to slide through options"

  • Presses clear button first to clear out any settings someone might have left.
  • Prefers quick-start functions like +30 secrather than setting a specific time.
  • Finds presets interesting but does not trust them, usually sticks to simple heating.

Reflections & Takeaways

This study highlights how users interact with a seemingly simple interface and where design improvements could be made:

  • Providing better descriptions for preset functions can increase trust.
  • Making power level adjustments clearer can improve usability.
  • Users appreciate quick and simple controls, so improving button layouts is essential.
